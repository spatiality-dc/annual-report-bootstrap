1. Inclusive Australia

How do we make Australia a more inclusive society?

Are you keen to help us create a more Inclusive Australia?

Forming an Alliance

The lyrics, ‘We are one, but we are many’, from the popular song ‘I am Australian’ speaks to our ambition to be an inclusive society; one where all people, regardless of race, religion, sexual orientation, age, gender or disability can fully contribute and feel accepted.

Keeping the values of respect and fairness at the heart of Australian culture is the aim of Inclusive Australia (IA), a new alliance formed by concerned organisations and individuals around the country.

To begin its work, IA needed data on where we’re doing well and where we’re falling down. That’s where we came in. IA asked our BehaviourWorks Australia team to conduct a foundational survey to provide a benchmark from which to monitor our progress towards an inclusive Australia.

Benchmarking our Progress

Since 2017, the team has surveyed more than 6,000 Australians for their views and experiences of discrimination. We asked how much contact participants had with people from minority groups and the nature of that contact, how they felt about belonging and wellbeing and how willing they were to volunteer or advocate on behalf of minorities. We also sought out minority groups to ask about their experiences of discrimination and prejudice.

Our results, published in Australia’s first-ever Social Inclusion Index, suggest that while we are not a highly prejudiced nation, there is room for improvement. However, almost a quarter of Australians experienced a major form of discrimination such as being unfairly denied a job and 27 percent experienced some form of ‘everyday’ discrimination, such as being called names. Critically, people who experienced discrimination had a lower sense of wellbeing and weaker identification with Australia than those who had not.

The Index will now be updated annually, with work also commencing in 2019 on a unique Diversity and Inclusion Tool to help business managers reduce discrimination in the workplace.

2. Climate Change and Health

How does climate change impact health and how do we address it?

We’re working across Monash to address climate change challenges to health in Australia and the region.

Understanding the impacts

Most people accept that climate change is transforming the global atmosphere and environment, but far fewer people understand the significant effects that climate change and environmental change are already having on human health. In our region alone, changing weather patterns, environmental degradation and natural disasters like bushfires, are causing more deaths, illnesses and injuries.

In 2019, MSDI contributed to the landmark Global Health Alliance (GHA) report From Townsville to Tuvalu. The report, which received widespread media coverage, was produced in collaboration with the Monash School of Public Health and Preventive Medicine and GHA member organisations. It frames the urgent regional challenges in planetary health terms and offers a roadmap forward.

Recommendations for change

Using evidence and case studies, From Townsville to Tuvalu shows how, for example, increased air and industrial pollution is linked to pulmonary and cardiovascular disease and how floods are increasing the spread of disease.

As contributing authors to the report, MSDI’s Associate Professor Annette Bos, Monash’s Finkel Professor of Global Health, Professor Jane Fisher and MSDI Director, Professor Tony Capon advanced discussions on the need for Australia to build bonds with the Asia-Pacific and how our aid, health and agricultural budgets can be used to build resilience. The authors made nine recommendations for Australian governments, including introducing a benchmark health survey, training health workforces and consulting deeply with health ministers in the Pacific before investing.

The report was widely discussed in the media, with MSDI Chair, Professor John Thwaites, making several mainstream media appearances to talk about the urgency of the issue and the cross-sector interventions required.

3. Economic Abuse & Domestic Violence

How do we prevent financial abuse in relationships?

Can you recognise the warning signs of financial abuse?

Recognising the warning signs

Financial abuse, also known as economic abuse, is a form of family violence. It happens when a partner, relative or carer intentionally uses finances as a weapon to control another person. A common tactic is debt abuse, where the victim is forced to take out a loan, or maintain repayments, and may have the asset withheld from them.

The Royal Commission into Misconduct in the Banking, Superannuation and Financial Services Industry did not examine financial abuse, but it did say that banks may be enabling abuse and that the sector was failing to adequately recognise the warning signs.

To make progress on the issue, which affects up to 16 percent of women and seven percent of men, MSDI worked with specialists in the Monash faculties of Law and Arts to find insights the Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC) could turn into action.

Review of the evidence

Led by MSDI’s Associate Professor of Law, Becky Batagol, the review sought to ascertain current levels of knowledge and explore how various forms of financial abuse play out in the real world. The project, which involved a literature review and a review of a decade’s worth of court decisions, as well as stakeholder and expert consultations, explored the social and emotional impacts of financial abuse on victims and how survivors typically raise concerns with financial institutions.

The team delivered their report to ASIC in December. It sets out the options for making the sector ‘financial abuse aware’ and reducing the harm to consumers who have experienced financial abuse. The key mechanism proposed was the introduction of mandatory financial abuse policies for all lenders, mortgage brokers and debt collectors. The report recognises the crucial economic and social roles that financial services play in our lives and how they form part of our communal response to two of our most pressing social problems: family violence and elder abuse.

4. Sustainable Riyadh

How do we turn an old city into a sustainable modern metropolis?

Are you interested in helping to transform cities for the future?

Transforming Riyadh Sustainably

Riyadh, the capital of Saudi Arabia, has been attracting merchants and immigrants since the 14th Century. Over the past two decades, however, the city’s population has grown rapidly and is now expected to double from seven to 14 million by 2030.

The city is located in the middle of the desert on the Arabian Peninsula and natural resources are limited, so the need for sustainable approaches to things like water and energy is acute. The kingdom also wants to diversify its economy away from oil and invest in major liveability initiatives so that Riyadh can remain a resilient global hub - and an inspiring place to live.

Evaluating the Plan

MSDI has been working with the city’s leaders since 2015, after the Arriyadh Development Authority (now the Royal Commission for Riyadh City) invited former MSDI Director, Professor David Griggs, to evaluate the city’s environmental plan. Joining him was BehaviourWorks Director, Professor Liam Smith.

The MSDI team reviewed 50 programs organised under five themes (air pollution, waste, water management, natural resources and open areas and wildlife), making 110 recommendations on how to improve, escalate or continue existing programs using behaviour change and other approaches. We also introduced a new climate change theme.

Reviewing the Results and a New Plan

In September 2019, our team returned to undertake a second review and see how our 2015 recommendations had been adopted and implemented. This time, we evaluated 311 sub-programs and made more recommendations for the next plan, including the introduction of a biodiversity theme.

In November, we delivered our findings, and also assisted with the planning for and chairing of, the Riyadh Sustainable Future Symposium. Our team are now in discussion with city leaders about a range of possible future projects focusing on climate change and planning for the 2020 G20 Summit.

5. Victorian Managed Insurance Authority Research and Innovation Program

How do we reduce preventable harm in our public health system?

Talk to us about the BehaviourWorks’ Method and how to design and implement trials.

Preventing harm

Victorians enjoy a world-class public health system with great facilities, highly-trained staff and a culture of compassion. But in any complex health system where numerous decisions are made under pressure, things can and do go wrong. The causes are many and varied, but human behaviour plays a part.

To examine the potential of behaviour change to reduce preventable harm in healthcare, the Victorian Managed Insurance Authority (VMIA) – the state’s insurer of health and other government services – funded our BehaviourWorks Australia (BWA) team to collaborate with it and The Shannon Company on a three-year Research and Innovation Program.

The research team worked with a range of stakeholders, including the Victorian Department of Health and Human Services, Safer Care Victoria and representatives of hospitals, emergency services and other groups to identify priority health challenges, which we’re seeking to address via seven simple, scalable behaviour change interventions.

The Patient Journey

The seven trials were conducted at various points along the patient journey where people intersect with the health system. Three of these trials were conducted in 2019 with the program set to conclude in early 2020.

Key findings include:

  • Simulating difficult conversations with a trained actor improved the confidence of health service board members to tackle difficult issues in the boardroom,
  • A ‘Rapid Diagnostic Discussion’ (RaDD) tool in Emergency Departments altered almost 25 percent of working diagnoses of abdominal pain, including serious problems initially thought to be minor ailments,
  • Providing patients with a simple, co-designed notepad enabled them to engage more in their healthcare and notified staff of safety and care issues they otherwise would have missed, and
  • A ‘choose your own adventure’ training video showed promise, with clinicians reporting less reluctance and more confidence to escalate care following training.

The program has involved some of Victoria’s largest hospitals and staff who were engaged to participate via a series of creative communication pieces. The learnings from this program will be shared with thousands of health workers and with MSDI teams working on other health projects.
