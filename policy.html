1. SDSN Australia NZ Pacific

How do we mobilise cross-sector action to achieve the Sustainable Development Goals?

Do you want to be part of the solutions? Join the SDSN network and take part in our activities.

The SDGs need everyone

To achieve the SDGs, we need all sectors and actors to do their part and work collaboratively to tackle the world’s most pressing sustainable development challenges. Since 2013 MSDI, as host of the UN Sustainable Development Solutions Network (SDSN) Regional Network for Australia, New Zealand and Pacific, has been leading efforts to mobilise support and action towards SDG implementation in Australia, regionally and globally.

With its core membership of knowledge institutions, and with local and global cross-sector collaborators, the network coordinates regional activities, strengthens university engagement with the SDGs and mobilises youth action on the SDGs. The network co-led the Australian SDGs Summits (2016 and 2018), has produced the globally-renowned guide Getting Started with the SDGs in Universities and supported the global establishment of SDSN Youth, among leadership activities.

Mobilising and coordinating action

To highlight and strengthen the critical role that young people play in achieving the SDGs, this year we supported an SDSN Youth Forum with the Foundation for Young Australians. It brought university students and representatives from youth-led and youth-focused organisations together to discuss progress, achievements and future ambitions.

With our Australian SDG Summit co-leads, the Australian Council for International Development, Australian Council of Social Service, Global Compact Network Australia and UN Association of Australia, we also issued a joint statement calling for Australian Government leadership on the SDGs and the five most important actions that need to be taken.

In 2019, we also launched Accelerating SDGs Practice, an online series of forums to help SDSN members accelerate and scale up action on the SDGs within their own institutions. The forums, run in collaboration with Australasian Campuses Towards Sustainability (ACTS), covered topics such as SDG mapping, engaging students with the SDGs and the Times Higher Education Impact Rankings.

2. Social Systems Evidence

How do we ensure policy makers have access to relevant, high-quality research?

Access the world’s most comprehensive repository of review-level research pertaining to all 17 Sustainable Development Goals.

Linking evidence to policy

The need for high-quality research on ‘what works’ to address global challenges is becoming more urgent as the 2030 deadline for the Sustainable Development Goals nears. However, finding credible research on the impact of different programs and policies can be challenging, especially for time-poor policymakers.

The Social Systems Evidence (SSE) platform is addressing this long-standing problem by providing free and fast access to a comprehensive repository of quality-appraised research reviews. We’re cataloguing the evidence-base on programs and policy options to help decision-makers decide which option is best to address Sustainable Development challenges in their part of the world. In 2019, MSDI entered into a partnership with McMaster University in Canada to advance this ground-breaking resource.

Cataloguing the research

MSDI has been finding, cataloguing and appraising the quality of research reviews relating to SDGs 7 (Affordable and Clean Energy), 13 (Climate Action), 14 (Life Below Water) and 15 (Life on Land). Monash University has considerable content-area expertise in these SDG areas, including through our ClimateWorks Australia team. In 2019, the Social Systems Evidence team developed a classification framework to enable users to easily find research on SDGs 7, 13, 14 and 15. We added 350 records to the database using this framework.

Following a global launch of the SSE platform in New York in September 2019, we also began working on rapid reviews that draw upon the platform to address real-world policy challenges. Topics include conservation management in Australia, recycling behaviours and business uptake of circular economy approaches (see MSDI’s Waste and Circular Economy Collaboration).

The SSE platform now has over 4,000 records. Users can save searches and receive email updates on areas of interest.

3. Sustainable Development Education

How do we create the sustainable development leaders of the future?

Do you want to help create a more sustainable future?

Building blocks for a better world

To realise the sustainable development goals, and secure the health and wellbeing of future generations, we need leaders with the ability to tackle the complex global issues we face. Our most urgent problems – like climate change and inequality – require solutions that are creative, collaborative and innovative. They demand passionate leaders who can inspire change across multiple disciplines, sectors, cultures and industries. That’s why MSDI is helping build the capacity of people and organisations to respond to the world’s greatest environmental, economic and social challenges.

Award-winning Masters

In 2019, the Monash Masters of Environment and Sustainability was awarded the Banksia Foundation Award for Research and Academia. A collaboration between the Faculties of Science and Arts, Monash Business School and MSDI, the Masters was recognised for its novel approach to interdisciplinary education. It’s an achievement reflected in the growing demand for the course, with enrolments increasing by 390% since 2017.

One of the units, ENS 5520 Understanding Human Behaviour to Influence Change was awarded a ‘purple letter’ for excellence for the second year in a row, recognised as outstanding by students and one of the top-rated units offered across Monash University.

Award-winning educators

MSDI’s Education team was also recognised for its excellence in the 2019 Monash Vice-Chancellor’s Education and Research Awards. MSDI’s Gitanjali Bedi, Associate Professor Annette Bos, and David Robertson all received awards for their outstanding contributions to sustainability education and training.

Collaborating to create real-world impact

MSDI’s Leadership for Sustainable Development specialisation in the Masters of Environment and Sustainability continued to grow in 2019 and play an important role in empowering our future sustainable development leaders. As part of the Masters, 25 students had the opportunity to address a real-world sustainability challenge through our Interdisciplinary Projects for Sustainable Development unit. The students completed six projects with industry partners including Australia Post, our BehaviourWorks Australia team, Telstra, the Global Health Alliance, Corporate Citizenship, and the South East Councils Climate Change Alliance. The unit is a great example of the close collaboration between Monash and our industry partners. It included field trips to help students better understand local conditions; and students also had the opportunity to present to the senior management forums of our industry partners.

Green Steps to a better world

MSDI’s award-winning Green Steps program also went from strength to strength in 2019, achieving a record number of applications. As part of the application process, students took over 150 personal sustainability actions, which they posted on our Take One Step platform. Thirty emerging sustainability leaders were offered a place on the program and given the opportunity to undertake a sustainability-related consultancy project with Monash’s Buildings and Property Division. Students were involved in six consultancy projects, addressing a number of sustainability issues across the University. This included minimising packaging waste from laboratory consumables and exploring the best ways to educate and engage students on sustainability and the Sustainable Development Goals.

Upon finishing the program, over 80% of students said they were more likely to pursue a career related to sustainability. Now in its 19th year, Green Steps supports students to develop the skills, tools and knowledge they need to become effective global leaders.

Fostering business leaders of the future

In 2019, MSDI’s Education team offered three units in partnership with the Monash Business School.

  • We launched a new core unit as part of the Master of Global Business and Master of Management, which scored very highly in overall student satisfaction.
  • Sustainability in a Global World was offered as a two-day seminar to 44 participants as part of the Masters of Business Administration.
  • We also offered a final year capstone unit Sustainability Practice and Organisations. Over 120 students from diverse international backgrounds completed the unit, with one student saying it had changed the way they thought about business and what its aim should be.

Building capacity in climate change and health

Our Education team also collaborated on research with the Faculty of Medicine, Nursing and Health Sciences to build capacity for the health profession to teach students about the links between health, and climate change and environmental sustainability

Professional and Continuing Education

In 2019 MSDI continued to bring the latest sustainable development and behaviour change thinking into the workplace with customised skills development, capacity building and executive training.

Bringing behavioural science into the workplace

In 2019, our BehaviourWorks Australia team engaged with 1160 people at 20 organisations, running 77 training and education sessions across the country. They ran behaviour change courses for the Australia and New Zealand School of Government in Brisbane, Hobart, and Wellington. Our team continued working with the Victorian Department of Premier and Cabinet to deliver training sessions to members of the Victorian public service, introducing behaviour change methods, how to design interventions, working up their own projects in the Behaviour Change Project Lab, and running randomised control trials. The team partnered with NSW Environment Protection Authority (EPA) to develop a 12-week practical, applied, project-based ‘Behaviour Change Bootcamp’ which has generated significant interest and excellent results. The team also designed and delivered a responsive, highly tailored curriculum of just-in-time training to support development of a new behavioural insights team at QBE Insurance. The training supported the team across the full project life cycle to design, implement and assess field trials.

Training for the BehaviourWorks Australia Consortium partners included masterclasses, specialist sessions around the INSPIRE tool for improving written communication and a new fully online behaviour change foundation course.

Helping businesses deal with climate change and business risk

In 2019 MSDI’s Education and ClimateWorks Australia teams collaborated on a Climate Change and Business Risk course for professionals and executives. Delivered for the first time in 2019, the course supported business leaders to assess the risks and opportunities arising from climate change and to apply these to their own company strategies. The training featured eminent guest presenters from industry, including from BHP, APRA, Westpac, IFM Investors and Minter Ellison. A total of 38 business leaders attended from across banking, investment, insurance, mining, retail, water, and engineering. As a result of the training, our experts also produced tailored workshops for senior company managers to inform climate strategy development.

MSDI also launched its Public Purpose Leadership program in December 2019. The program builds on the legacy of the Centre for Sustainability Leadership Future Makers program, which was passed to MSDI when the Centre closed its operations. The program has been postponed due to COVID-19, but will be re-scheduled once restrictions have eased.

5. Leave No One Behind

How do we build innovation and entrepreneurial skills to improve social inclusion?

Use your degree to change the world and be part of our next cohort.

Fostering social innovation

Leave No One Behind is one of the largest university social entrepreneurship programs in Australia. Led by MSDI and run in collaboration with the Faculties of Arts, Law, Education, Business, and Art, Design and Architecture, the program gives Monash students the opportunity to address social inclusion challenges in the community by developing social business ideas. The program is also building critical skills, behaviours and mindsets needed for entrepreneurship and social impact. Over 10 weeks, students work in interdisciplinary teams to develop, test and pitch new business ideas. Winners are awarded seed funding and in-kind support to help get their businesses off the ground.

Leave No One Behind has generated many powerful ideas over the past three years and alumni have gone on to access other entrepreneurship opportunities at Monash and beyond, building a pipeline of future leaders and innovators.

Creating future innovators and entrepreneurs

Leave No One Behind kicks off with a Social Innovation Summit, an inspiring day where students learn about the Sustainable Development Goals and hear from social entrepreneurs making a difference. In 2019, 250 students attended, with 100 offered a place in the program.

Seven teams consisting of 32 students then worked to develop, test and pitch ideas at the final showcase evening. Winners included Aunty, a social enterprise that makes and home-delivers spice kits, and donates proceeds to service providers supporting and empowering migrant women. Slumber was another winner, producing an aromatic pillow spray that creates a sleep-inducing environment. Profits from this go towards helping rough sleepers in Victoria.

The 2019 People’s Choice Award went to M189, a web-based tool designed to help international students keep track of the changing requirements of Permanent Residency Immigration Points.

Supporting past winners

Past winners also continued to be supported by MSDI in 2019 through mentorship and in-kind support – including, Assisted Outing (2017), an online platform that takes people with various needs on local and global outings that is currently expanding into China, and One Love (2018), a social business that inspires and educates high school students about the impact of racism and social exclusion.

6. Behaviour Change Graduate Research Industry Partnership

How do we train the next generation of behavioural experts?

Are you interested in supporting the next generation of behaviour change experts? Talk to us about how you can get involved.

Translating insights into practice

Just a decade ago, the ‘behavioural sciences’ were viewed as a disparate mix of social science disciplines. Today, specialised behaviour change units are an integrated function of many government agencies. This rapid expansion has led to strong demand for PhD qualified behavioural experts capable of straddling the worlds of academia and industry. It’s a gap that MSDI has sought to fill by establishing the first-ever Behaviour Change Graduate Research Industry Partnership (GRIP) program. The program utilises our BehaviourWorks Australia team’s world-recognised expertise in solving real-world problems and working with government and industry to translate behavioural insights into policy and practice.

Monash’s first behaviour change GRIP

Eight industry partners are now working with 17 talented PhD candidates on a series of common and complex policy problems. This includes domestic violence, plastics use, energy performance, environmental and ecosystems management, road safety, mental health, social inclusion and healthcare.

Working together as a cohort, and with the support of supervisors from six Monash University faculties and institutes, in 2019 the candidates gained a strong grounding in behavioural theory and practice. They were guided through a professional development program and completed a series of industry placements to learn about their respective partners’ strategic priorities. The candidates also successfully achieved their key milestones and gained confidence presenting at, and participating in, various formal and informal events, including the 3 Minute Thesis competition, ‘Nudgeathons’ and scientific meetings.

Many students also began to find their voice, penning articles for The Conversation and other media outlets and accepting invitations to participate in local and international workshops. The combined efforts, outputs and achievements of the students were shared at a dedicated GRIP Showcase in December 2019.

7. ANZSOG Report -"Today’s Problems, Yesterday’s Toolkit"

What are the new methods governments are embracing to solve public problems, and how do we promote them?

Are you interested in assessing the innovation capabilities of your government agency?

Innovating in the public sector

Governments of all political stripes are being buffeted by technological and societal change. As public problems become more complex, governments are struggling to tackle them effectively and legitimately. To do so, the public sector needs to embrace new problem-solving tools and rethink processes that inhibit innovation. But do Australian public servants have the capabilities needed to solve public problems? And are public sector leaders enabling or disabling this? In 2019, we set out to answer this question.

Fostering a new generation of public problem solvers

In collaboration with the Governance Lab at New York University, and funded by the Australian and New Zealand School of Government (ANZSOG), we surveyed over 400 Australian and New Zealand public servants in a pioneering study. We asked policy practitioners about their awareness, competency and use of a range of new innovation methods. While all of these methods are being used well somewhere in government, and people are eager to embrace them, training is not prioritised by government agencies. We published our findings in a landmark report entitled: ‘Today’s Problems, Yesterday’s Toolkit’.

The report outlines why public problem solving is so urgent at this moment in history and pinpoints the new skills that ‘public entrepreneurs’ need. This includes systems thinking, data analytics and behaviour change. We studied effective public sector training programs around the world and identified key features to inform future course development. The report was widely promoted in the media and public service, and was ANZSOG’s most downloaded report for the year.

MSDI is now extending its partnership with ANZSOG and The GovLab to design and develop an online course which will help public servants use new innovation methods to solve complex public problems. We’re also advising government agencies on these methods for public problem solving. And we’ll be supporting government agencies to identify their capability gaps and co-designing training programs to address them.

8. McKinnon Institute for Political Leadership

How do we enable better political leadership in Australia?

If you’re an MP, learn more about The McKinnon Institute and the courses on offer.

Enhancing the effectiveness of our political leadership

Politicians operate in an increasingly complex environment, yet politics is one of few vocations without structured professional development. The McKinnon Institute is a new organisation dedicated to providing professional development programs for state and federal politicians. Non-partisan and not-for-profit, the Institute was established in June 2019 through a partnership between Monash University and the Susan McKinnon Foundation. The Institute sits within the portfolio of the Deputy Vice-Chancellor for Education, Professor Susan Elliott and has significant input from MSDI. The Institute is committed to raising the standard of political leadership by offering professional development for current and aspiring politicians. It will also work to widen the pool of talented people who would consider a career in politics and build their capability, confidence and impact.

Relationship building and course development

In 2019 MSDI worked with Monash External Relations Development and Alumni to formalise a partnership between Monash University and the Susan McKinnon Foundation. MSDI then worked with the DVC (Education) to support the Board in developing strategy and governance, liaising with senior stakeholders and driving course development. The team reviewed global best practice in leadership development and conducted extensive stakeholder-based research to inform course development. Interviews, focus groups and surveys were conducted with a variety of stakeholders, including state and federal MPs, to gain an understanding of their needs and interests. Strong relationships were established with the Speaker of the Federal Parliament and Speakers and Presidents of some state Parliaments.

The Institute is now in the process of developing and delivering a range of programs, including broad-based training for current politicians and a more selective program to develop advanced leadership capabilities. The McKinnon Institute for Political Leadership benefits from the strong partnership MSDI enjoys with the Monash Professional and Continuing Education (PACE) team.
