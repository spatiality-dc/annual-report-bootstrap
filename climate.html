1. Land Use Futures

How can we sustainably feed a growing population while managing the impacts of climate change?

Are you a funder who's passionate about feeding the world?

Accelerating global action

Over half of Australia’s land is utilised by agriculture, which contributes significantly to the economy. However, agriculture, forestry and other land uses are responsible for around 12% of Australia’s emissions, and over half of Australia’s water consumption. With global population increasing, the world will need to feed another three billion people by 2050. Without significant shifts in consumption patterns, enormous pressure will be placed on land and oceans – which are already in decline.

For Australia to maintain a competitive food and land use sector, meet the needs of future generations and realise economic opportunities, agriculture needs to become more sustainable and resilient. To achieve this, the Land Use Futures program is working with the international Food and Land Use Coalition, a global effort to accelerate action towards sustainable food and land use systems by 2050.

Partnering for impact

In partnership with Deakin University and CSIRO, the Land Use Futures program aims to support the transition to a sustainable food and land use system in Australia. The program takes a participatory approach to problem solving, inviting leaders from the private sector, government, research and community to co-create pathways for a range of possible solutions across dietary shifts, conservation, waste, circular economy and food innovation. These solutions could deliver improvements for the climate, environment, livelihoods, food security and nutrition. The program aligns with the Sustainable Development Goals, particularly Goal #2: Zero hunger.

Natural Capital Summit and Roadmap

In 2019, the Land Use Futures team ran workshops around Australia, to understand the challenges the sector faces. With the support of the Queensland Government and NAB, the team delivered the Natural Capital Summit in Brisbane, where 150 stakeholders were engaged in further discussions. From this, they identified the need for an evidence-based, practical framework for linking financial outcomes and land management practices.

Our ClimateWorks team created the Natural Capital Roadmap, which outlines action areas for improving the measurement and valuation of natural capital in Australia. In 2020, the Land Use Futures program also contributed to the Food, Agriculture, Biodiversity, Land-use and Energy Consortium report, Pathways to Sustainable Land-Use and Food Systems. The team also launched the Natural Capital Investment Initiative in partnership with NAB, to help farmers in adopting sustainable and profitable land management practices.

2. Net Zero Momentum Tracker

How do we hold businesses accountable for their emissions?

Do you run a business and want to be part of the transition? Pledge to be net zero by or before 2050.

Capturing the commitments

As the climate heats up, many Australian businesses are committing to helping Australia lower its emissions, in line with the Paris Climate Agreement. But how do we know what these commitments are?

This year, led by our ClimateWorks Australia team, we developed the Net Zero Momentum Tracker, which gathers Australian emissions reduction commitments together in one place. Our tracker captures the depth of commitments made, from general commitments to net zero pledges, to increase transparency around corporate climate action. The project also provides opportunities for our team to engage with sector leaders to understand the challenges they face and how our research can support decision-making.

Tracking the property sector

Given how influential the property sector is in terms of energy use and emissions, our first report released in 2019 examined the commitments made by 21 property companies (those listed in the ASX200 and those with the highest reported emissions). We found that 43 percent of these companies had a target, aspiration, or are on a path closely aligned with net zero by or before 2050. 47.5 percent of companies were taking steps to reduce emissions. Two companies had no apparent emissions reduction commitments or activities.

Tracking the banking sector

In November 2019, our second report found that 25 percent of the 20 banks assessed were preparing to target net zero emissions by or before 2050 for their investment and lending portfolios. A further 65 percent were fully aligned or on a pathway to net zero by or before 2050 for their operations, which includes energy use in offices and branches. The team has also published reports in 2020 assessing net zero commitments in the retail and local government sectors.

3. Whole of Economy

How do we transition to net zero economies?

Do you want to be part of the transition?

Creating system transformation

Building on a decade’s worth of work to achieve a sustainable, low-emissions future, MSDI’s ClimateWorks Australia team is collaborating with government and sector leaders to create the system transformations required to bring emissions from Australia, Southeast Asia and the Pacific in line with global climate targets. We’re also assisting decision-makers across the region to identify low-carbon forms of economic growth.

Developing leadership

In 2019 MSDI, together with our ClimateWorks team, delivered our first executive education program: Climate Change and Business Risk - Developing a Strategic Approach. The course educates business leaders in identifying climate change risks and opportunities, developing and climate scenarios and applying this understanding to company strategy.

Creating a vision for the future

In 2019, our team also worked with CSIRO on the question: What will Australia look like economically, socially and environmentally by 2060? The outcome of this investigation is the Australian National Outlook 2019, a comprehensive body of work that identifies our most significant economic, social and environmental challenges across five key areas: industry, cities, energy production, agriculture and corporate culture. The report warns of a slow decline if no action is taken. However, it paints a bright future if challenges are addressed early.

With Climate-KIC, our ClimateWorks team also jointly hosted ‘Unlocking Australia’s Sustainable Finance’, involving practitioners from the European Union’s High-Level Expert Group on Sustainable Finance. In addition, the team also partnered with Vivid Economics to consider how Indonesia, the Philippines and Vietnam can take advantage of growing low-carbon markets.

4. Victorians Act for Nature

How do we encourage Victorians to act for nature?

You can do something every day for nature.

Gaining a better understanding

Victorians Value Nature (VVN) is a program run by the Victorian Department of Environment, Land, Water and Planning (DELWP) as a key plank of the Victorian Government’s Biodiversity 2037 plan. It aims to raise awareness of biodiversity and facilitate occasions for people to connect with, and act for, nature.

Many Victorians already connect with nature in their daily lives and these behaviours can easily be incorporated into programs such as VNN. To gain a better understanding of how Victorians connect with nature day-to-day, our BehaviourWorks Australia team collaborated with DELWP in 2019 on the design and implementation of a foundational survey with 3,000 Victorians.

We uncovered a deep connection to nature

Our survey revealed that 95 percent of Victorians are aware of the condition of Victoria’s environment and the importance of biodiversity. Some 86 percent have pro-environmental and pro-social values. Over 60 percent feel connected, or very connected, to nature, and 32 percent spend time in nature every day or every other day. The top three places where Victorians felt most connected included national parks, beaches and their own gardens. In fact, 42 percent told us they frequently spend time in their garden. But we also found some common barriers, including not having enough time, having family and friends who don’t enjoy spending time in nature and gaining access.

Developing a new plan

The findings of the Victorians Value Nature survey are now being considered by DELWP to help increase opportunities for Victorians to connect with nature. This includes through their own gardens and by performing small acts, such as planting native species, participating in citizen science and picking up litter on the beach.
